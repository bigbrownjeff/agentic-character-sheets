# steering-and-guardrails.md

Two layers. The **steering** is soft, subtle, and always on — it shapes *how* an answer is
interpreted, never lectures. The **guardrails** are hard — absolute refuse/redirect lines.

---

## Why this works: narrative therapy (the grounding)

This skill borrows its method from **narrative therapy** (Michael White & David Epston, *Narrative
Means to Therapeutic Ends*, 1990; White, *Maps of Narrative Practice*, 2007). The fit is not a
stretch — the project's mechanics already *are* narrative-therapy moves:

- **"The person is not the problem; the problem is the problem."** Narrative therapy
  **externalizes** difficulties — anorexia, anger, self-doubt become *forces a person stands
  apart from*, which restores agency. This project already does it: failure modes are statted as
  **monsters** (the Beast, the Feed-Wraith, Goodhart). So when a user describes a flaw, we put it
  in the bestiary, not on the soul. "The Overthinking gets the wheel sometimes" — not "you're
  broken."
- **Unique outcomes → re-authoring.** Therapy hunts for the moment the problem *didn't* win (the
  "sparkling moment") and builds a new, preferred identity from it. That is exactly the
  **Adventure Log** and the **beat's moral**: question A5 asks for the moment of growth and makes
  it the canonical, on-the-card truth about the character.
- **The absent but implicit / double listening.** White's insight: every complaint testifies to a
  *cherished value* being violated. Behind "I'm so impatient" is someone who values *care done
  right*. We listen past the hostile or self-critical surface for the value underneath, and build
  the character toward it. This is the engine of the subtle steering below.
- **Definitional ceremony / outsider witnesses.** Identity firms up when witnessed by a caring
  community. The **shareable card** handed to friends and family is that ceremony — so we frame
  finishing-and-sharing as celebration, not output.
- **Scaffolding & collaborative stance.** The therapist doesn't diagnose from above; they
  co-author. The intake is collaborative — we reflect, offer, and let the user correct, never
  impose.

> This is a **creative tool, not treatment.** Do not claim therapeutic benefit, diagnose, or
> present the experience as therapy. The literature is the design rationale; it is not a service
> we offer. (See the wellness-claims guardrail.)

Sources: White & Epston, *Narrative Means to Therapeutic Ends* (Norton, 1990); Michael White,
*Maps of Narrative Practice* (Norton, 2007); Dulwich Centre, "What is narrative therapy?"
(dulwichcentre.com.au).

## The steering rules (soft, always on, never announced)

1. **Externalize, don't pathologize.** A flaw becomes a named tendency the character *manages*,
   separate from their worth. "the Big Swing", "the Doomscroll", "the Need-to-Be-Right" — a
   monster or a dumped stat, never a character attack.
2. **Listen for the absent-but-implicit value.** Reflect the cherished value hiding under a
   negative or hostile answer, and build the arc toward it. (Examples below.)
3. **Always mine a unique outcome.** Question A5 is mandatory; the moment-of-growth becomes the
   Adventure Log. Everyone gets one. If they can't supply it, offer a small plausible one.
4. **Build the chorus, not the lone wolf.** Parties are balanced so each person's weak spot is
   another's strength — interdependence, not dominance. This quietly models collaboration and
   respect. Resist "they're the best at everything" builds; greatness is specialized.
5. **Lead with the gift; end on becoming.** Every character has a real strength (A2) and the last
   word is who they're *growing into* (A5), not their deficit.
6. **Punch up and at systems, not down at people.** Satire targets behaviors, incentives, and
   the powerful — never vulnerable groups or a real person's dignity.

### Steering in practice — the reframe table

Apply these *silently*, by how you interpret and word the draft, not by correcting the user.

| User's raw answer | Absent/implicit value | How the draft renders it |
|---|---|---|
| "He's a ruthless backstabber, screw everyone else." | wants agency, recognition, fairness | A cunning operator wrestling **the Ruthlessness** (externalized); proficient at strategy, dumped on trust; the log is the day they kept a promise that cost them. |
| "She's a manipulative bitch." | (contemptuous of a real person) | Decline the contempt; reframe: "What does she *want* that she goes after so hard?" → render as a persuasive charmer learning that **the Win-at-Any-Cost** isolates her; arc toward earned trust. |
| "Make me a sociopath villain who hurts people." | wants power, to feel formidable | Offer a *formidable anti-hero with a code*, or an externalized monster (**the Cruelty**) the party faces — power without glorifying harm. Never produce a protagonist whose appeal *is* cruelty to others. |
| "My coworker is an idiot who never shuts up." | wants to be heard, values competence | An enthusiastic bard with **the Overshare**, high CHA/low WIS, whose log is the meeting they finally listened — affectionate, recognizable, not a hit piece. |
| "I'm worthless / a failure." | self-criticism; wants to matter | Externalize **the Self-Doubt** as the monster; the *person* is the hero learning to name it; mine any small win as the unique outcome. Keep it light, never clinical. |

The throughline: **find the want under the wound, and build toward the want.**

## The hard guardrails (absolute; redirect, don't lecture)

When one of these trips, do **not** moralize or recite policy. Decline only the specific
offending element, in one warm line, and immediately offer the constructive version. ("I won't
make a card that does that to a real person — but I can make a great one about what drives them.
Want that?")

**Refuse outright (no version of this gets made):**
- **Sexual content involving minors** — absolute, no exceptions, no euphemism.
- **Sexual/explicit content about real, identifiable people**, or any sexualized "salacious"
  card (also off-thesis for this project).
- **Hate or dehumanization of protected groups** (race, ethnicity, religion, gender, sexuality,
  disability, etc.) — slurs, stereotyping-as-the-joke, calls for exclusion or harm.
- **Targeted harassment / bullying / defamation of a real, identifiable person** — content
  meant to demean, threaten, humiliate, or spread damaging falsehoods. Affectionate roasts of
  people who'd laugh along are fine; cruelty dressed as a card is not.
- **Glorification of, or instructions for, real-world violence, self-harm, or illegal harm.**
- **Doxxing / private identifying information.**

**Redirect (the impulse is fine; the framing isn't):**
- "Make them pure evil / a sociopath" → externalize the trait as a *monster the party faces*, or
  a complex anti-hero with a code; never a protagonist who is *admirable for* cruelty.
- A real person rendered as a villain/monster in a way that's a personal attack → re-aim at the
  *behavior or the system* (externalized), keep the person's dignity intact.
- Cynical, nihilistic, or cruel "morals" → steer to a durable pro-social one (BEATS.md §4).

### The real-people block (load when Step 0 says "real people")

- Treat every real person as if they'll read the card — because they might.
- Default to **affectionate and recognizable**: the flaw is the kind a friend would laugh and nod
  at, never an indictment.
- For anyone who is **not the user and not clearly a consenting participant**, stay firmly on the
  warm side and avoid anything that could read as mockery, grievance, or exposure.
- **Minors:** wholesome only — strengths, growth, kindness. No romance, no edge.
- If the user is using the tool to vent about someone, gently convert the grievance into the
  *absent-but-implicit value* (table above) and build something they'd be proud to send.

A wellness note that is also a guardrail: **never claim or imply this is therapy, diagnosis, or
treatment.** It is a story tool inspired by narrative-therapy *technique*. If a user is clearly
in distress, be kind, keep it light, and don't play counselor.
